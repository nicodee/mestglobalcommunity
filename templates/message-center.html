Useful tips

Always fill in the subject line with a topic that means something to your reader. Not "Deadline" or "Important!" but "Revised deadline for product update."

Put your main point in the opening sentence. Most readers won't stick around for a surprise ending.

Never begin a message with a vague "This." As in, "This needs to be done by 5:00." Always specify what you're writing about.

Don't use ALL CAPITALS - no shouting! Also, don’t use all lower-case letters.

As a general rule, PLZ avoid textspeak (abbreviations and acronyms): you may be ROFLOL (rolling on the floor laughing out loud), but your reader may be left wondering WUWT (what's up with that).

Be brief and polite.

If your message runs longer than two or three short paragraphs, consider (a) reducing the message, or (b) providing an attachment.

Remember to say "Please" and "Thank you."

Add a signature block with appropriate contact information (in most cases, your name, business address, and phone number, along with a legal disclaimer if required by your company).

Important: edit and proofread before hitting "send." You may think you're too busy to sweat the small stuff, but your reader will think you're careless.

Reply promptly to serious messages. If you need more than 24 hours to collect information or make a decision, send a brief response explaining the delay.

Start with a salutation. e.g. Dear Mr. Jones, or Dear Professor Smith for someone you don’t know well, especially if they’re a superior, and Dear Joe, or Dear Mandy if you have a working relationship with the person.

Stick to one topic for each email.

Use simple English.

Always, always proofread email before sending!

Link to referenced documents or resources whenever possible. Don’t simply say “Kindly refer to the marketing plan document.” Instead, provide a link to said document (or include it as an attachment). Make things as easy as possible for your reader.

Hi Jack,

I’m Kwame, co-founder and CTO of Jhello, an incubator company at the Meltwater Entrepreneurial School of Technology. I’m writing to ask your advice on how to scale our technical infrastructure.

Jhello is a private social network for members of the African diaspora. We’re growing very rapidly, and we’re having trouble scaling our infrastructure. Considering your experience scaling infrastructure for multiple companies, I would love to get your perspective on how to do this most effectively.

Would it be possible to Skype for an hour sometime next week to discuss strategies that might help us deal with this?

Regards,
Kwame

Follow up email sample

Hi Beth,

Hope you’re doing well. Thanks once again for the session you had with the team last week. It was extremely helpful. We’ve started implementing most of the things we discussed:
